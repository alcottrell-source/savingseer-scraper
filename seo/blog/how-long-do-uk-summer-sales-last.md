Short answer: **about six weeks, from late June to early August** — but the useful answer is that "how long" is the wrong question. A summer sale isn't the same thing from start to finish, and the week you visit matters far more than whether the sale is technically "on".

## The typical timeline

From the sale cycles we've tracked and verified across UK shopping centres, the summer season breaks down roughly like this:

- **Late June:** the first movers. A handful of brands start early — often fashion chains clearing mid-season stock. Centres read quiet-to-rising. Plenty of signs, not much substance yet.
- **Early July:** the build. Shops join day by day and the share of a centre genuinely on sale climbs fast. This is when "the summer sales have started" headlines appear — and when the discounts are still at their shallowest.
- **Mid-to-late July:** the peak. Most centres hit their widest point — the largest share of shops on sale at once — and then **hold there**. Crucially, this is also when sales *deepen*: shops that opened at "up to 50%" push the same rails to 60% and 70% as the weeks pass. Widest choice, deepest cuts, stock still in reasonable shape.
- **Early August:** the wind-down. Shops drop back to full price one by one as autumn ranges land. What remains is often the deepest-discounted stock of the whole season, but it's the remnants — broken sizes, last season's colours.

Individual brands run shorter cycles inside that window — two to four weeks is common — which is why a centre's sale can outlast any single shop's. The centre-wide sale is really dozens of overlapping brand sales, each on its own curve. (For the fuller picture of how that curve works, see [the anatomy of a sale cycle](/blog/anatomy-of-a-sale-cycle).)

## Why the dates drift

No two years are identical. A cold June delays clearance; a weak trading spring brings it forward — a retailer sitting on unsold stock doesn't wait for the calendar. Some years the season stretches deep into August; others it's effectively done by the first week. That drift is exactly why a date-range answer ("late June to early August") will sometimes steer you wrong by a fortnight in either direction.

The other complication: the season doesn't arrive everywhere at once. Different centres peak on different days depending on their brand mix — a centre heavy in the early-mover fashion chains crests before one anchored by department stores.

## How to know where your centre is right now

You can't read a sale's age from the high street — a window that says SALE looks the same on day 2 and day 32. What tells you is the shape of the activity:

- **The share of shops on sale, and its direction.** Climbing means the sale is building; holding near its high means you're at the widest point; easing means it's receding.
- **Whether discounts are still deepening.** Mid-cycle percentage bumps ("was 50%, now 70%") are the signature of a sale at its peak plateau — the strongest combination of choice and depth.
