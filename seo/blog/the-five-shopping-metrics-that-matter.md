Walk through any shopping centre and you'll be shouted at by signals. SALE in a window. "Up to 70% off" on a banner. "The summer sales are here" on the radio. And almost none of it tells you the one thing you actually want to know: **is this trip worth it?**

A window poster is one shop's claim about itself. A banner's "up to" is doing a lot of work. And the calendar tells you when sales are *supposed* to happen, not whether anything is happening today, here, in the shops you'd actually walk into.

After building Tide, I've stopped looking at all of that. I check five numbers instead.

## 1. How much of the centre is actually on sale

This is the big one, and it's the number the whole of Tide is built around: **the share of a centre's shops that are genuinely on sale today.** Not "has a sign up". On sale, verified.

It reads like *23 of 40 shops on sale*, and that's a fact you can check, not a vibe. It's also how you should compare centres: the one where the most shops are actually discounting wins, not the one with the loudest banners. Two centres ten minutes apart can be sitting at 12% and 55% on the same Saturday. Same "sale season". Completely different trips.

I walked through a real example of this number in [What a sale actually looks like](/blog/reading-the-tide-card-at-high-tide) — it's the difference between a wasted Saturday and a good one.

## 2. Whether that number is rising

Here's the part most people miss: **sales build, they don't switch on.**

The first day of a "sale" is often the worst day to go — a few keen brands, shallow discounts. A week later it's spread across the centre and the prices have dropped further. So a centre at 30% *and climbing* is a very different story from a centre at 30% on the way back down.

That's why the second number I check is the direction: *up from 12% last week*. A rising number means the event hasn't peaked yet. A falling one means you're shopping the leftovers. The [sale calendar](/blog/uk-sale-calendar-2026) can tell you the season; only the trend tells you the week.

## 3. How fresh the markdowns are

A centre can hold a high score for weeks, but day 18 of a sale is not day 3. The best sizes go early. What's left gets picked over.

So the third number is age: **when did each sale start, and when did its discount last change?** That second part matters more than it sounds. Shops routinely deepen a running sale — 50% off quietly becomes 60% off three weeks in — and when they do, it's effectively a fresh event: new price, and often new stock pulled onto the rails to go with it. A sale that started this morning and an old sale that got deepened yesterday are both worth your attention. A sale that's been sitting untouched at 40% for a fortnight usually isn't.

## 4. How deep the discounts go

Twenty-three shops at 10% off and twenty-three shops at 60% off are the same score and completely different days out. One is token clearance rails; the other is the kind of event you plan a Saturday around.

So alongside *how many*, I check *how much*: the actual percentage each shop is running, and — the underrated bit — **how that compares to the deepest that shop has ever gone.** A brand that historically bottoms out at 60% offering 30% today isn't done yet. A brand at its all-time deepest is as good as it gets. That's the honest way to answer "is this discount actually good?" without taking the banner's word for it.

## 5. How many of those shops are *yours*

The quiet killer of all four numbers above: none of them matter if the sale isn't in shops you'd buy from. A centre at 60% is irrelevant to you if your brands are the ones sitting at full price.

Tell Tide which shops you actually buy from and every centre gets read against *your* list: **9 of your 14 shops on sale**, with the names spelled out. Same score, same centre — but now it's your answer, not the crowd's. And when one of your shops starts a sale, you get told that morning instead of finding out from a window three weeks late.

## Five questions, one decision

That's the whole system. How much of the centre is on sale. Which way it's heading. How fresh the markdowns are. How deep they go. And how much of it is *yours*.

Notice none of those tell you what to do — Tide is descriptive almost everywhere, on purpose. There's exactly one exception: when a centre peaks with enough of it in your shops, the card says **go now**. It's rare, so it means something.

The rest of the time, the five numbers just answer the only question worth asking before you set off: **is it worth going today?** Pick your centre, check today's score, and stop letting window posters make that call for you.
